# ADR-004: Split Semantic Coordination from Mechanical Scheduling

## Status

Accepted

## Date

2026-04-25

## Context

A purely deterministic scheduler can safely maintain idle-agent state, wake sessions when matching work exists, sweep expired leases, and recover claim state. It cannot understand user intent, decompose complex goals, judge worker output quality, or synthesize final results. A coordinator-agent can make those semantic decisions, but it should not own operational safety primitives such as atomic claim, lease expiry, heartbeat, concurrency limits, or crash recovery.

The orchestration research in `docs/ideas/orchestration/` supports a layered model: distributed-systems mechanics in the daemon and semantic orchestration in a coordinator.

## Decision

Use a combined model:

- A coordinator-agent owns semantic orchestration: decomposition, child-task creation, delegation intent, result validation, follow-up task creation, and synthesis.
- A daemon-owned mechanical scheduler owns operational safety: idle registry, capability-aware wakeups, lease sweep, recovery, backpressure signals, and deterministic state transitions.
- `ClaimNextRun` remains the single authoritative claim primitive. In the MVP, the scheduler does not claim runs directly; the owning agent session or explicit operator assignment path initiates the token-fenced claim.
- Manual user-created tasks and user-started sessions remain peers of autonomous work and use the same task/run/session contracts.
- Coordinated task runs use a task-bound coordination channel for coordinator/worker communication. That channel is not an ownership or terminal-status authority; claim, heartbeat, complete, fail, and release remain task service operations.

The coordinator-agent may create tasks, express requirements, post coordination messages, request handoffs, and synthesize results. It must not bypass `ClaimNextRun`, leases, spawn limits, channel permission checks, or task state transitions.

## Alternatives Considered

### Alternative 1: Daemon scheduler controls all orchestration

- **Description**: The daemon alone decides work decomposition and assignment through deterministic rules.
- **Pros**: Highly testable and predictable.
- **Cons**: Poor semantic quality for complex goals and no synthesis capability.
- **Why rejected**: The desired system needs autonomous decomposition and reasoning, not only queue placement.

### Alternative 2: Coordinator-agent controls all scheduling

- **Description**: The coordinator-agent chooses workers and tracks ownership directly.
- **Pros**: Flexible and easy to evolve through prompting.
- **Cons**: Makes safety, liveness, and recovery depend on an LLM.
- **Why rejected**: Claim fencing, leases, crash recovery, and concurrency limits must be deterministic.

### Alternative 3: Scheduler claims and pushes every run

- **Description**: The daemon scheduler selects a run, claims it, and pushes it to a chosen session.
- **Pros**: Centralized placement decisions.
- **Cons**: Competes with agent-pull verbs and manual operator assignment, creating two ownership paths.
- **Why rejected**: The MVP should have one claim authority: `ClaimNextRun` called by the owning session or explicit operator path.

## Consequences

### Positive

- Balances semantic quality with operational safety.
- Keeps state transitions testable.
- Allows coordinator-agent behavior to evolve without compromising task ownership.
- Avoids duplicate claim paths between scheduler push and agent pull.
- Preserves manual user control.

### Negative

- Adds two cooperating components instead of one.
- Requires clear boundaries so coordinator and scheduler do not duplicate decisions.

### Risks

- Risk: coordinator, scheduler, channel messages, and manual users disagree on what should run.
- Mitigation: coordinator and users express desired work as tasks/metadata; channel messages are operational conversation only; the task service remains the only durable ownership authority through `ClaimNextRun` and token-fenced transitions.

## Implementation Notes

The scheduler should be an in-daemon goroutine wired from `internal/daemon`. Its MVP responsibilities are rebuildable idle registry, lease sweep, boot recovery, wake notifications, and observability. The coordinator-agent should be a normal managed session with restricted orchestration tools, task-run coordination channel access, and workflow correlation.
